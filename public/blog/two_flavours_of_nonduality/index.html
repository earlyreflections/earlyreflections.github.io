Two Flavours of Non-Duality

2025-09-08

[A] dualism: a distinction drawn in terms that make essential relations between the distinguished items unintelligible.—Robert Brandom1

There is not the slightest difference between saṃsāra and nirvāṇa. There is not the slightest difference between nirvāṇa and saṃsāra.—Nāgārjuna2

Duality is an ambiguous word. Sometimes when we speak of duality we may just be indicating the presence of a difference. But at others we may intend something more specific and weighty, that distinctively metaphysical kind of difference more directly evoked by the term ‘dualism.’ These two senses of duality correspond to two ways of understanding subject-object duality and what it might mean to overcome it. The first sees it as a matter of overcoming the illusion of difference between subject and object. The second locates the illusion elsewhere, in whatever it is that makes a real difference between subject and object show up as a false dualism.

The first wants to understand the true relationship between subject and object on the model of metaphysical identity. In the final analysis subject and object are unified in a single, ultimate substrate of reality, conceived of as undifferentiated in itself. According to this view subject-object duality manifests as a kind of cognitive veil, a conceptual net projected over the raw experience of reality, carving it up into all sorts of illusory differences, including the perceiving subject’s own difference from the reality it perceives. The experience of non-duality is imagined as a lifting or piercing of this veil, a cessation of conceptual activity in which the perceiving subject attains direct experience of undifferentiated reality, and consequently experiences itself as undifferentiated from it.

The second framework appeals to the notion of reification. According to this view subject and object are indeed different, in the sense that there is no single underlying substance or substrate in which they are ultimately unified. What is at issue in subject-object duality is rather a tendency we have to reify subject and object—to posit them as distinct substances—not just in our theories but also in our cognitive reflexes. The practical significance of this reification is that subject and object are treated as self-subsisting things which could in principle exist independently. But according to this picture the relation between them is in truth a relation of co-constitution, and in this respect they are logically inseparable. The corresponding experience of non-duality is one in which the reifying cognitive reflex is suspended, in which subject and object can show up in consciousness without automatically being imputed with the kind of intrinsic substantiality that makes thinkable their coming apart.

The first view is often associated with Advaita Vedanta, while the second is recognisably Buddhist. How non-duality is actually understood within these traditions is a complex issue, and is not something I want to get into in this post. The aim here is just to contrast two distinct models that hover in the background of discussions of non-duality—sometimes in a shadowy kind of way—without getting too bogged down in hermeneutics. I’ll refer to them simply as the identity model and the reification model.

The reification model is certainly harder to get a grip on than the identity model, but there are clear tensions between the two. Firstly, the reification model recognises the reality of difference, while the identity model posits that all differences are illusory from the perspective of ultimate reality. The reification model therefore avoids the trouble that the identity model is going to have explaining the difference between everyday truth and everyday falsehood, since if conceptual cognition always involves imposing a false difference then this implies that all cognition is in error. Secondly, the identity model doesn’t actually address the issue of reification—it merely displaces it by positing a single reified substance in which the difference of subject and object is annulled. If the reification of reality presents a problem in itself, over and above its tendency to harden differences into dualisms, the identity model will have to answer to it.

Given that something is indeed at stake in the difference between these two frameworks, how might we get a better grasp on the reification model and the corresponding experience of non-duality? I find it useful to think of this in terms of a theatre analogy. In naturalistic forms of theatre the universe depicted on stage is presented as a self-contained totality. The audience exist at an Archimedean point outside it, looking in on a world that cannot look back. This separation between the world of the stage and that of the audience is a kind of reification, not purely cognitive in this case but a product of social convention, helped along by lighting and stage design. This artifice conspires to construct the world of the stage as self-subsistent and separate, bracketing the true state of affairs: that audience and stage are constituted as such only in their relationship to one another.

But this situation can be disrupted, as when a character addresses the audience directly. By breaking the fourth wall the character in effect places a demand on the audience to recognise them as a social agent within their world, undermining its separation from the world of the stage. This moment contains a glimpse of what it means for a structure of reification to collapse, and so what this experience of non-duality might really be like. This analogy encourages us to imagine subject-object duality as something like a staging effect, one that occurs in the process of cognition itself, where the construction of the object of perception as a distinct self-subsisting unity is just the other face of the displacement of the perceiving subject to an observational viewpoint outside its sphere of involvement. The construction of the world as ‘objective’ (in the sense of being articulated into distinct self-subsisting objects) is at the same time an artificial suspension of the subject’s participation within it.

Still we might wonder, how exactly does this picture differ from the identity model? After all, breaking a fourth wall sounds remarkably similar to piercing a veil. But the important difference is that breaking a fourth wall is not about coming into some kind of ‘direct’ contact with the object, insofar as ‘direct’ means unmediated by concepts, as if the character were to dissolve into a pure flux of consciousness the moment they addressed you. The person on stage remains as they are, their attributes left in tact. What has been disrupted is the background of convention which positions them in their role.

In this experience of non-duality we should expect no change in the content of cognition, only a modification of its form. If we can think of this as a species of illusion, then it is not the kind in which the object appears other than it really is. It is instead the kind of illusion which, once shed, only reveals the object as it always had been. Not the lifting of the veil to reveal what is behind it, but the realisation that the veil was never there to begin with.

To put it like this is to risk recycling a platitude, but I hope these reflections can help put some meat on the bones of this old cliché. If subject-object duality is realised not by a conceptual veil that obscures the true reality, but through an artificial displacement of the subject to a position outside the world of the object, then the experience of non-duality need not involve some kind of unification with undifferentiated ultimate reality, or indeed anything so lofty. Instead it will consist in something more subtle: the loss of a certain observational distance, after which the subject will find themselves for the first time in the world they have always occupied, with all its familiar differentiations. Only this time it will be a world in which they are involved not accidentally but essentially, in virtue of their very constitution.
