Note on the Tension Theory of Craving

2026-05-14

What I’m calling a ‘tension theory of craving’ here is something I’d imagine is familiar to anyone who’s spent any time in Buddhist meditation circles. It is the idea that craving and aversion are manifestations of sanskaras understood as pockets of trapped tension.

The attraction of this idea is clear, and it certainly chimes with the basic meditative experience. Cravings arise as contractions of mind and body; by noticing them, disidentifying with them, and relaxing around them the meditator encourages them to dissipate their energy. But the tension theory goes a step beyond this to claim that this relaxing around tension is itself the releasing of the sanskara. This is a claim I’ve heard most often in vipassana and other neo-Theravada contexts, sometimes leading people to see a close affinity between Buddhist meditation and Western practices such as somatic experiencing and TRE, which aim to induce various kinds of somatic release in the treatment of trauma.

What I’d like to point out is that we don’t need to see sanskaras this way at all. In the first instance a sanskara is a habit—a disposition to act on craving conditioned by past behaviour, a disposition which only becomes active when it comes into contact with particular triggers. It is certainly true that tension will accompany the triggering of a sanskara and the consequent arising of craving, and moreover that relaxing around this tension can be of great use in arresting the reflexive identification with craving that empowers it as action.

But this does not mean the tension was somehow there all along, and nor does it mean that relaxing around the craving will make it go away. Sometimes it does, of course—this is often the case with small hinderances that arise in meditation. Less so for the bigger cravings and aversions, when much stronger psychological forces are in play. Often the reality is that no matter how much we relax around contraction, sitting with craving can be extremely uncomfortable. This is OK, because ultimately it is not something waiting to be released, but a conditional response that is being reconditioned. Discomfort should be expected, perhaps lots of it. But if you have a tension theory in the back of your mind, the persistance of discomfort even in the relaxation of all apparent tension could easily become confusing or disheartening.

Ultimately the tension theory seems to reify sanskaras. But sanskaras are not concrete things, not pockets of stuff trapped somewhere in our psyches. They are latent in our minds only in the sense that a disposition to melt is latent in a block of iron—the disposition becomes active (iron melts, craving arises) only in response to contact with a triggering stimulus. This is just a brief note, but something I wanted to jot down as it highlights both the subtle way in which reifying tendencies can infiltrate the understanding of meditative experience, and some of the practical consequences of this.
