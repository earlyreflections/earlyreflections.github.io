The Three Turnings in Classical Yogacara

2025-12-21

One of the key differences between the early Buddhist and later Mahayana teachings is the way they each portray the relationship of the path to its destination. The early teachings stress that in its natural state the mind is obscured by very real afflictions, and the path is accordingly presented as the effort to draw up a map of the afflicted mind and to then use it to systematically cut away the fetters at the root of suffering. Nirvana is achieved only once this active and strenuous process has been completed, a world-transcending state which marks a definitive exit from samsara.

In contrast, Mahayana traditions tend to stress that the mind is always already awakened, and construe the path as a process of coming to realise this. They are suspicious of modes of practice which treat the afflictions as substantially real, worrying that in doing to so they only reinforce the reifying tendency which inhibits realisation of the Buddha-nature which is already there. Instead they recommend practices based on not-doing, typically non-dual or open awareness practices which aim at a certain ‘abiding in thusness’ without conceptualisation (tantric practices depart from this in some ways, but inhabit the same overall framework). Samsara already is nirvana—your job is to notice that.

The Buddha’s own comments can be ambiguous on this point, as for example when he likens the path to the purification of gold. On the one hand the gold is already there. But on the other hand so are the impurities, and their removal implies a process of real change. How different are these two presentations, really? The question feels live for me, as I currently seem to be converging on a mode of practice which is essentially neo-Theravada insight meditation, but with a view of the Dharma which is informed primarily by Mahayana philosophy. On the one hand I find the detailed and systematic approach of insight meditation generates enthusiasm and curiosity in me in way that non-dual practices simply do not, at least at this stage of my practice. Even if you accept that the aim is to stop doing something, rather than to do something, you still need to have a grasp on what exactly it is that you’re aiming to stop doing. Or to use another of the Buddha’s similes, if the Dharma is likened to a raft then you can accept that the raft is just a tool for getting to the other shore, not an end in itself, while also acknowledging that you do in fact need a raft and can’t just teleport across the river.

Yet at the same time I remain sceptical of world-transcending interpretations of nirvana, and do agree that this model generates meta-ethical problems that can’t be resolved without embracing a more thorough-going philosophy of emptiness and something like the Bodhisattva ideal (though I’d perhaps add the caveat that exactly how these innovations solve those problems is far less obvious than generally acknowledged).

Squaring this circle has been one of the main factors driving me to explore Yogacara more deeply. Following on from Ben Connelley’s book on Vasubandhu’s Thirty Verses, which I discussed previously, I’ve been reading William Waldron’s superb Making Sense of Mind-Only, which is significantly more comprehensive and historical in scope. Like Connelly, Waldron rejects the interpretation of classical Yogacara (which includes the Yogacara sutras along with the classic expositions of Vasubandhu and Asanga in the 4th or 5th century) as a metaphysical idealism. Instead he sees it as a form of cognitive constructivism, more concerned with how experience arises as a function of the interaction between our environment and our mental faculties than with fundamental ontology.

Of particular interest here is the Yogacara teaching on the “three turnings of the wheel of Dharma,” a framework through which Yogacara synthesises the detailed psychological maps of earlier Buddhism with the new Mahayana emptiness teachings found in the Perfection of Wisdom sutras and their explication by Nagarjuna. The first turning refers to both the early Buddhist teachings as presented in the Pali discourses, and their systematisation in the various Abhidharma schools that followed.

According to the Abhidharma analysis all experience can be broken down into constitutive elements called (confusingly) dharmas, point-like moments of awareness constantly flashing in and out of existence. All the normal objects of our experience—medium-sized dry goods, thoughts, sensations, our sense of self—are just higher-level patternings of these momentary dharmas, appearing with a false solidity in roughly the same way that moving objects appear on a film screen despite being constituted by a rapid sequence of static frames. One of the features of many Abhidharma systems is that the dharmas are the only entities considered to ultimately exist, while everything else exists only in some derivative sense.

The second turning refers to the Mahayana emptiness teachings, and its distinctive innovation is that it rejects the ultimate reality of dharmas. A consequence of this stance is that it flattens the difference in status between the dharmas and aggregate phenomena, which are now all viewed as equally empty of essence or substantial reality. One lesson that could be taken from this, which later comes to define the Madhyamaka school, is that Buddhist theorists should stop trying to construct positive accounts of the functioning of the mind and reorient themselves toward the deconstruction of any and all views that treat—explicitly or implicitly—some entity or class of entities as non-empty.

While Madhyamaka could be described as an anti-metaphysics, it remains in a metaphysical register in the sense that its purely negative style of reasoning aims to deconstruct the fallacy of reification that underlies all positive metaphysical views, a fallacy it takes to be the primary barrier to liberation. In light of this it is perhaps not surprising that Madhyamaka philosophers like Candrakirti, writing several hundred years after Asanga and Vasubandhu, often construed Yogacara as a metaphysical idealism only to then accuse it of reifying the mind.

Waldron argues that Yogacara is better seen as a different kind of response to the emptiness teachings, one which can first be approached by considering the limitations of Madhyamaka. In restricting itself to purely deconstructive discourse, Madhyamaka deprives itself of the resources needed to answer certain kinds of questions. For example, Madhyamaka agrees that there is such a thing as conventional truth and hence that there is a difference between conventional truth and conventional falsehood. But at the same time it seems to preclude the possiblity of our ever being able to say what this difference consists in. The Madhyamika seems committed to a kind of quietism about the nature of conventional truth—the best they could say is that if you want to know what mathematical truth is, ask a mathematician; if you want to know what historical truth is, ask a historian; but there is no way of getting at an overarching, domain-neutral theory of conventional truth which the univocity of the concept seems to demand. This is not inconsistent, but it is certainly unsatisfying. The Madhyamika might say that the difference is just that some conventional beliefs are useful while others are not, but a statement like that remains vacuous in the absence of an account of what it is that might make a conventional belief useful in general. And it is exactly that kind of account that Madhyamaka forbids itself from offering.

Furthermore, from the point of view of liberation the issue is not really that we believe phenomena to non-empty. The issue is that we perceive them to be so, and this may be the case regardless of what we believe. If we are to free ourselves of our ingrained perception of the world as infused with illusory essence, what we’d really like is a substantial account of why it is that our mental faculties automatically impute essences to phenomena which do not in fact possess them. Once again, here Madhyamaka can only maintain a principled silence. But for Yogacara this is the key question, and what allows it to take it up as a positive program is precisely its reframing of the emptiness teachings in cognitive rather than metaphysical terms. This is the move which defines the third turning of the wheel of Dharma.

For Yogacarins, then, the relationship to the Abhidharma looks like this. As per the emptiness teachings they reject the ultimate reality of the dharmas, but they do not take this as reason to reject the Abhidharma framework as a whole. Instead they reinterpret the dharmas as something more like epistemological primitives than ontological primitives—as building blocks of a theory of mind rather than building blocks of reality. This allows them to use those aspects of the Abhidharma which successfully explain our mental patterns while augmenting them with extra elements which could help to explain those aspects of mind which the Abhidharma system struggled to accommodate. For instance in another of its signature teachings, Yogacara posits an unconscious layer of mental processing to account for memory and karmic continuity, neither of which can easily be made sense of in a system in which the only components are momentary, sequential, and manifestly present dharmas.

What all this points to is a way of integrating the Mahayana emptiness teachings with a form of practice that is continuous with, rather than divergent from, insight practices rooted in the substantial content of the first turning psychology. Without seeing these as descriptions of substantially real afflictions one can nevertheless actively engage them as necessary tools—as in the simile of the raft—in coming to see exactly how the illusion of substantiality is staged, and which can be discarded once they have served their purpose.
