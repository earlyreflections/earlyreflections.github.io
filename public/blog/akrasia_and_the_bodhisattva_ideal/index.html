Akrasia and the Bodhisattva Ideal

2026-02-10

A familiar stereotype underlying the pejorative ‘Hinayana’ framing of the non-Mahayana schools is the monk who withdraws from the world to selfishly pursue enlightenment for themselves. According to this framing the Mahayana distinguishes itself from the Hinayana through its emphasis on compassion as the primary goal of the path.

This stereotype is undermined somewhat by the fact that even according to the most world-transcending early portrayals of nirvana, the cultivation of moral perfection, which certainly includes compassion, is regarded as necessary for enlightenment. Any monk who was genuinely withdrawn from the suffering of others would thereby be incapable of achieving enlightenment, by Hinayana lights too. In terms of outcomes, then, there appears to be little difference between the two vehicles.

Nevertheless there is clearly something at stake in the Boddhisattva ideal. My view is that the dispute really concerns a more foundational metaethical question, namely: why should we be moral at all? If the Hinayana answer is that you should cultivate moral concern for others because it will help you achieve enlightenment, then the Mahayana could be seen as rejecting this answer, instead urging that moral concern must be cultivated as an end in itself.

Given outcomes are the same, you could be forgiven for wondering why this question matters. But while theoretically the destination may be the same, the answer may have important implications for how moral motivation works in practice. Indeed, the whole reason it might seem like a good idea to find self-interested reasons to cultivate moral virtue is that they provide a practitioner with concrete, personal reasons to pursue a moral project which may otherwise seem abstract. Perhaps, then, we can see the Boddhisattva ideal as arising out of a growing suspicion that this strategy is flawed. More explicitly, the suspicion might be that self-interested motivations for cultivating moral virtue are self-defeating.

Why might we believe this? One immediate reason is that life routinely throws up situations in which self-interest and moral concern are at odds with one another. Even armed with the observation that the cultivation of moral concern makes us happier (a fact now confirmed by empirical psychology), this logic has limits—many situations arise in which morality demands a degree of self-sacrifice impossible to justify in terms of some hypothetical renumeration in happiness or spiritual growth. In fact it is only in these situations we’d typically consider there to be something genuinely moral on the line.

But there’s another, more fundamental reason to reject self-interested justifications for morality. To illustrate, consider a response to the above problem based on a conception of ‘expanded’ self-interest. We might think here of Vedic conceptions of self in which one’s own personal identity is equated (via the intermediary notion of Atman) with the whole of reality or Brahman. On this view moral concern for others is justified on the basis that they already are you, in some suitably foundational and metaphysical sense. From the perspective of the ultimate, their needs and preferences are subsumed into your own sphere of prudential self-interest.

This route may seem promising, as it seems to offer a simple and direct reason to care about the suffering of others. But consider how it might regard conflicts of interest. Since according to this model a conflict of interest between two people is (from the ultimate perspective) a conflict between two parts of the same self, the situation is analogous to a conflict of desire within a single person. Say I decide to quit smoking on Monday, then on Friday find myself reaching for a cigarette. How do I decide which desire to act on? One approach is to suggest there can only be one ‘true’ desire, and resolving the conflict is simply a matter of improving one’s self-knowledge. But this amounts to the claim that the conflict is illusory, a position which if dubious in the case of conflicting desire will only become more so when widened to moral conflict in general.

Another common approach is to view resolution of the conflict as a matter of will-power. But this misunderstands the problem, which is not about the relative strength of the conflicting desires. It is rather a logical problem arising from the fact that there is only one locus of account. By recognising my desire to stop smoking on Monday I effectively place myself under a rule: to refrain from smoking even if the desire arises. The problem is that the same authority with which I bind myself to that rule can then be used to nullify or reinterpret it later. So when the desire to smoke inevitably does arise I have no real reason to discount it in favour of my original desire to quit. Viewed this way, it can be seen that the problem is not so much that my capacity to self-legislate is too weak, but that it is too strong. In practice, if the force of a reason (to act or refrain from acting) derives from immediate self-legislation then it seems we will never have good reason to do anything other than act on whichever desire is immediately present. This is regardless of whether it conflicts with other desires we’ve had at other times, or with higher aspirations we may associate with our sense of ourselves as beings extended in time.

This has sometimes been called the problem of akrasia. This word was first used by philosophers in ancient Greece who were perplexed about how it is possible that a person could ever act against their better judgement. But what the above line of thought actually points to is the opposite problem, namely how it is possible that anyone ever restrains their immediate impulses in the pursuit of long term goals. The lesson usually taken from this argument—which originated with Wittgenstein and was later developed by Saul Kripke1—is that the normative force of a rule, and hence of reasons in general, always depends on its being articulated within a community of rule followers. If there is only a single rule follower the content of the rule will be inherently unstable. What seems to be required is the recognition of others’ authority to hold you to the commitments you take upon yourself.

This insight into the social nature of rule-following and the normative force of reasons both shows why an Atman-based account of expanded self-interest cannot provide an adequate response to the metaethical question, and points the way to a better answer. The problem is that by assimilating moral concern into the prudential sphere of a single universal self it generates a problem analogous to akrasia, in effect guaranteeing that genuine moral conflicts can never be resolved in practice (parties to a conflict can never provide one another with a reason to compromise). But while it shows that self-interest cannot be used to underpin moral concern, even when universalised, it also hints that the opposite may be possible. If our capacity to take ourselves as autonomous rational subjects always depends on our embedding within a community who mutually reinforce and stabilise the content of the rules we each place on ourselves, then this indicates that something like a moral sense—the mutual recognition of distinct others as ends in themselves—is logically prior to any desires, preferences or goals indexed to individual, temporally extended selves.

Let’s come back to the Bodhisattva ideal. I’ve suggested that this development within the Buddhist tradition might be seen as the rejection of a certain answer to a distinctively metaethical question. But once looked at this way, we can also see that it doesn’t really provide its own answer. If asked why we should be moral, the Bodhisattva ideal just says yes. The line of thought I’ve been exploring here gestures towards a more substantial answer to this question. This answer is broadly Kantian: it says that the authority of morality derives directly from the structure of our own rational agency. More explicitly, it says that insofar as we take ourselves to have any reasons at all—including our own self-interested desires and preferences—we are already implicitly committed to moral reasons to act or refrain from acting, whether or not we acknowledge this commitment explicitly. The further (ultimately Hegelian) insight that practical agency is a social achievement makes explicit how the moral sense—the recognition of other sentient beings as sources of reasons which obligate your action—is logically prior to rational self-interest.

  1. Introduced in the Philosophical Investigations (1953), Wittgenstein first presented this as an argument intended to show that a language spoken by just one person is impossible. It was later developed into a general rule-following paradox by Kripke in Wittgenstein on Rules and Private Language (1982). ↩︎
