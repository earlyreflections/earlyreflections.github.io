Anattā and the Screen of Consciousness

2025-10-13

In this post I’d like to briefly consider a metaphor that comes up often in relation to consciousness, in many different contexts. Anyone who follows contemporary philosophy of mind will know how important the idea of a ‘screen of consciousness’ is for framing the big questions. It is often used to illustrate the hard problem of consciousness, for example, as it is in David Chalmer’s popular introduction where he asks us to consider the ‘inner cinema’ of conscious experience. There’s also a long line of physicalist thinkers from Richard Rorty to Daniel Dennett who have tried to deconstruct it, arguing that the screen is a bad metaphor generating false problems. Here I’ll consider it from the point of view of meditative experience and awakening, where I believe similar tensions are in play.

In the context of insight meditation, the screen of consciousness is sometimes used to describe the process of dis-identification from the various mental elements that constitute our experience of self. So for example, when a thought or desire arises in meditation we intend to notice it rather than identify with it—in doing so this mental formation moves from the ground of consciousness (where it would normally be pulling our strings) into the figure, where it appears as one more object on the screen of consciousness and where it can play out according to its own internal dynamics without our involvement.

When this reflex gets strong the effect can be quite dramatic. Once or twice I’ve had an experience where enough concentration, calm and equanimity have developed that arising elements start to automatically take on this objective, dis-identified quality, without any active noticing taking place. In these moments—which in my case have always been brief and unstable, just glimpses really—there’s a very strong sense of ‘zooming out’ as the entire field of consciousness becomes unified, each sensation, thought and volition appearing as one more brushstroke on the same canvas. In these moments consciousness really does seem screen-like, and the use of the metaphor to describe this process feels perfectly apt.

When I’ve discussed this experience with a teacher, they have said that what is being glimpsed in this sense of zooming out is the process of awakening itself, which involves precisely this disentangling of pure awareness from the constitutive elements of the self structure. But something about this explanation seems incomplete to me, at least insofar as awakening is concerned. The components of self are traditionally described by the five aggregates of clinging: form/matter (rūpa), feeling (vedanā), perception (saññā), mental formations (saṅkhārā), and awareness (viññāṇa). But in the zooming out process it seems to me that what is really happening is that dis-identification with the first four aggregates is being achieved only through a particularly strong kind of identification with the fifth.

The point I’m making here is just that as various aspects of the self move from the ground of consciousness into the figure, as awareness as such is disentangled from its identification with what it is aware of, the difference between figure and ground is not only left in tact but is actually reinforced. We can certainly imagine a limit point of this process, perhaps even accept that there is some threshold that once crossed ensures the zoomed-out-ness remains permanent. But is this really a good thing to aim for? What is left at this limit is a pure point of awareness—a subject without a self—peering out on a world no part of which it identifies with, and so in which it does not really participate. But this seems to me to be a subject which still clings to its sense of itself as a subject, and the objects of its awareness as things it is simply not involved with.

If we take seriously the idea that realising anattā involves letting go of the fifth aggregate—awareness itself—as well as the preceding four, then surely what will have to go is this whole imagery of a screen of consciousness. The effect of this metaphor is to license the idea of consciousness as constituting a separate, self-subsisting domain between the subject and world on the other side—an eye of the mind viewing the images on the screen of the inner cinema, which may or may not be accurate representations of the world outside. Logical problems aside, as far as Buddhism is concerned what’s important to note is that this picture treats consciousness as non-empty (it’s an underappreciated point about emptiness that it is supposed to apply as much to the inner as to the outer world). Whether we view it from the perspective of the five aggregates or through the philosophy of emptiness, the metaphor of the screen has to go.

What could it mean to let go of awareness itself? Well, perhaps the key thing here is that what must disappear is the very difference between the figure and ground of consciousness, and with it the sense of a dis-identified subject peering at an inner screen. What this gestures to is a radical inversion of the whole process, where the ‘zooming out’ turns into something more like a ‘plunging in.’ In the end the real lesson is perhaps not that it is possible to zoom all the way out, or that it is possible to do so permanently, but that the very possibility of zooming out shows that the figure/ground relationship is itself mutable and impermanent, and that the screen of consciousness is itself just another construct.
