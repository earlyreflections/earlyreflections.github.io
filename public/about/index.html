Welcome to Early Reflections, a space where I’ll be exploring issues in Buddhist philosophy. My own background is in Western philosophy of language, philosophy of mind and epistemology, with a special interest in the revival of Kant and Hegel within analytic philosophy. One of the central aims of this project is to read these traditions against each other.

I first came into contact with Buddhist philosophy when a thread of argument relating to illusionism and the hard problem of consciousness led me to Jay Garfield’s work on Nāgārjuna. From there I became interested in Madhyamaka, Yogācāra, the epistemology of Dharmakīrti, the various disputes between and syntheses of these schools, Abhidharma, and ultimately the whole philosophical milieu that Buddhism emerged from and exists in. It remains fascinating to me how so many of the questions that recur in Buddhist philosophy—especially in the Indian Mahayana—foreshadow the main themes of early modern European philosophy.

I am also a meditator. When I first started meditating it was not particularly connected with my interest in philosophy, Buddhist or otherwise. Over time, however, these two streams have converged as I have developed more familiarity with the Dharma and become increasingly persuaded that it offers a comprehensive and rational framework for living. The fragments posted here accordingly constitute one limb of a broader practice. They represent my own efforts to find my way about in the Dharma.

That said my aim here is to consider various facets of the Dharma through the unashamedly heterodox lens of Western analytic rationalism, not to give a faithful exposition of the Buddhist worldview. If it seems to me that Kant and the Buddha have a disagreement, I come with no prior commitments about who must win the argument. Then again I don’t believe either would want me to, and I find no inherent contradiction between the soteriological ambitions underlying the Buddhist cultivation of Right View and the more Promethean impulses that are part and parcel of the philosophical legacy we inherit from Greece. My only promise is that I shall try to be brief and not waffle on too much. Less brief than Nāgārjuna, perhaps, but certainly more so than Hegel.

Sam
